# EconVenture Level 2: The Product Line
### A design draft for the advanced journey

Level 1 taught one founder, one product, one market. Level 2 is the scale-up. You won your first market, and now the trap that catches most founders is in front of you: the market was never one blob of buyers. It is a stack of people who would each have paid a different price, and at your single price most of them walked away happy with a bargain you handed them for free. Level 2 is about learning to see that hidden money and to capture it without breaking the market or the law.

This draft assumes Level 1 carries over: same company (Lumen), same co-founder Reya Sodhi, the same rival Darius Vane returning at the top of the market. Level 2 unlocks only after the instructor opens it, so a class can finish and discuss every Level 1 concept first.

---

## The spine: one dramatic question for the whole level

> Every buyer who would have paid more than your price just got a discount on your dime. How much of that gap can you turn into profit, without wrecking the market or bringing the regulator down on you?

That single question runs through all six beats. Act 1 shows you the money you are leaving behind (consumer surplus and the deadweight loss of pricing above cost). Acts 2 and 3 teach the three ways to capture it (discriminate by buyer, discriminate by self-selection). Act 4 makes the segments move under your feet with the economy. Act 5 forces you to fund it all from a fixed pool of resources. The finale is the war where everything fires at once and a regulator watches how greedy you got.

---

## Design rules this build must respect

These are not style notes. They are the difference between a sharp teaching tool and an unplayable spreadsheet.

1. **One moving part per act.** When a stage teaches a concept, the other two product tiers are frozen or hidden. Three live products only appear in the finale. If a stage needs more than two sliders to make its point, the stage is wrong.
2. **The math stays transparent, but never all of it at once.** Each act shows the one formula it is teaching, with real numbers plugged in, live. It does not show three products' full cost and demand math on the same screen.
3. **Welfare is taught on a single product.** A three-product welfare diagram does not read. Consumer surplus and deadweight loss are drawn once, cleanly, on one curve in Act 1, and referred back to in words later.
4. **Name the concept precisely.** The three-tier line is second-degree price discrimination, framed as self-selection. First and third degree get their own act so the taxonomy is clean.
5. **Drama comes from stakes, not knobs.** The macro cycle and the regulator create tension. Added variables do not.

---

## The macro engine that drives the thrill

Level 1 had one-off shocks. Level 2 runs a full cycle as a visible character on screen: recovery, boom, peak, recession, repeat. The cycle is what makes three tiers worth having, because each tier reacts differently to income:

- **Premium (smart bulb):** a luxury. Income elasticity well above 1. It soars in the boom and collapses in the recession.
- **Mid:** roughly normal. Income elasticity near 1. Steady, and squeezed from both sides.
- **Basic:** income-inelastic, close to a necessity. Barely moves with income, so its share of your sales rises in a downturn as buyers trade down.

The player who reads the cycle and rotates the product mix wins. The player who pours capacity into premium right before a recession gets punished. That tension is the engine of the whole level.

## Cast

- **Reya Sodhi**, co-founder, your voice of cost discipline from Level 1.
- **Priya Nair**, the new segments analyst, who keeps showing you the buyers you cannot see.
- **Darius Vane**, the rival, now selling a rival premium bulb and reacting to your moves.
- **The Competition Commission**, the regulator that appears in the finale when your pricing gets too aggressive.

# Prologue: The blob was a lie

**Dramatic question:** if buyers are not all the same, why are you charging them all the same?

**The scene.** Priya drops a chart on the table. Your one demand curve, the friendly straight line from Level 1, is actually thousands of individual buyers stacked from the one who would pay a fortune down to the one who would only buy if it were nearly free. "You have been treating a crowd like a single customer," she says. "That is leaving money everywhere."

**The gameplay.** A demand curve the player can zoom into until it breaks into individual buyer dots, each with a willingness to pay. Drag a single price line across it and watch the crowd split into three groups in real time: buyers far above your price (delighted, barely noticed the cost), buyers just above it (the marginal ones), and buyers below it (lost sales). The first new word lands here: **willingness to pay**, and the gap between it and your price.

**Concept, stated plainly.** A demand curve is a ranking of willingness to pay. Market segmentation is the act of grouping buyers who behave alike. The crowd is not one customer; it is segments with different ceilings.

**Transparent formula.** For any price P, buyers served = everyone whose willingness to pay is at least P. The live readout: at P, you serve Q buyers and turn away the rest.

**The hook into Act 1.** Priya circles the buyers sitting far above your price. "Every one of these paid you less than they were willing to. Want to see exactly how much you gave away?"

# Act 1: The money on the table
### Consumer surplus, producer surplus, deadweight loss

**Dramatic question:** how much value does your pricing create, and how much does it quietly destroy?

**The scene.** Priya shades the chart. The triangle above your price and under the demand curve lights up. "That," she says, "is the buyers' bargain. Consumer surplus. Money they kept because you only charged one price." Then a second region lights up between your price and your marginal cost: your own gain, producer surplus. Then the gut punch: a third triangle to the right, the trades that never happened because you priced above cost. "That one is nobody's. It just vanishes. Economists call it deadweight loss."

**The gameplay.** A single price slider over one product. Three shaded regions update live as you drag: consumer surplus (buyer gain), producer surplus (your gain), and the deadweight loss triangle (welfare destroyed by restricting output below the competitive level). A running counter at the top reads "value created" and "value destroyed." Slide toward the competitive price and the deadweight loss shrinks to nothing while your producer surplus also shrinks. Slide toward the monopoly price and your slice fattens while the pie shrinks. The visceral lesson is in your hands on the slider.

**Concept, stated plainly.** Consumer surplus is what buyers gain over what they paid. Producer surplus is what you gain over your marginal cost. Deadweight loss is the value of the mutually beneficial trades that do not happen because price sits above marginal cost. Market power makes your slice bigger and the whole pie smaller.

**Transparent formula.** Consumer surplus is the area of the triangle, roughly half of (choke price minus price) times quantity. Producer surplus is roughly (price minus marginal cost) times quantity. Deadweight loss is the triangle between the quantity you sell and the larger quantity a competitive market would have reached. Each area prints its number live as the slider moves.

**The thrill mechanic.** A "left on the table" meter that fills with the consumer surplus you are not capturing. It sits there glowing for the rest of the level as the thing you are trying to claim.

**The hook into Act 2.** Reya: "What if the same bulb did not have to sell at the same price to everyone? What if the buyer who would pay a fortune actually paid one?"

# Act 2: One bulb, three prices
### Price discrimination, first and third degree

**Dramatic question:** if you could charge each buyer closer to their ceiling, how much of that lost triangle becomes yours?

**The scene.** Priya lays out the fantasy and the reality back to back. The fantasy: read every buyer's mind and charge each their exact maximum. The reality: you cannot read minds, and anyone you charge a low price can resell to the one you charged high.

**The gameplay, in two beats.**

*Beat one, the perfect-discrimination thought experiment.* A toggle that lets you charge every buyer exactly their willingness to pay. Watch the consumer surplus triangle drain completely into your producer surplus, and watch the deadweight loss vanish too, because now every beneficial trade happens. The screen shows the unsettling truth: perfect price discrimination is efficient (no waste) and ruthless (you take all the gains). Then the reality check fires: a resale event where a buyer you charged little flips bulbs to one you charged a lot, and your scheme collapses. Lesson: first-degree is the ceiling of what is possible and almost never achievable.

*Beat two, the realistic version.* Split the market into two identifiable segments Priya hands you: price-sensitive households and price-insensitive offices. Each has its own demand curve and its own elasticity. Set a price for each. The engine runs MR equals MC inside each segment separately, and your total profit jumps above the single-price result. The rule reveals itself on screen: charge more where demand is less elastic.

**Concept, stated plainly.** Price discrimination means charging different prices for the same good, not because cost differs. First degree charges each buyer their maximum (the efficient, impossible extreme). Third degree charges identifiable groups different prices based on their different elasticities. It needs three things: market power, separable segments, and no resale between them.

**Transparent formula.** The inverse-elasticity rule, shown live for each segment: the optimal markup over marginal cost is larger when elasticity is smaller. (Price minus marginal cost) over price equals one over elasticity, computed for each segment so the student sees why the office price ends up higher than the household price.

**The thrill mechanic.** A "fairness and backlash" gauge. Push the gap between segments too far and you trigger complaints, then press coverage, foreshadowing the regulator. Profit and reputation pull against each other.

**The hook into Act 3.** Priya: "Offices and households were easy because you could tell them apart. But most of your buyers refuse to wear a label. So what if you made them sort themselves?"

# Act 3: Good, better, best
### Second-degree price discrimination, versioning, cannibalization

**Dramatic question:** can you design a menu so buyers reveal who they are by what they choose?

**The scene.** This is where the three products are born, and not as a vanity product line. They are a trap, in the best sense. You cannot tell a high-value buyer from a low-value one by looking, so you build a basic, a mid, and a premium bulb and let buyers walk themselves into the right tier. The premium buyers reveal themselves by reaching for premium.

**The gameplay.** Design the ladder: set a price and a quality level for each of the three tiers. Watch buyers flow into tiers in real time as little streams on screen. The central tension is **cannibalization**, made physical: price the premium too low and you watch high-value buyers cascade down into it, gutting your margin. Make the basic too good and nobody climbs. A live "self-selection check" tells you whether each tier is actually pulling its intended buyers or stealing from the tier above. The counterintuitive trick lands here: sometimes you deliberately hold the basic back (the "damaged goods" idea) to protect the premium, and the game lets you feel why that can raise total profit.

**Concept, stated plainly.** Second-degree price discrimination sorts buyers by the version they choose rather than by who they are. It works when the menu is incentive compatible: each buyer prefers the tier you designed for them. Versioning is the everyday name. Cannibalization is the failure mode, where your own cheaper tier eats your dearer one.

**Transparent formula.** Cross-price elasticity, computed inside your own line and shown live: the percent change in mid-tier sales divided by the percent change in the premium price. A positive number means they are substitutes, which is exactly cannibalization, and the size of it tells you how dangerous a premium price cut is to your mid tier.

**The thrill mechanic.** A real-time flow visual of buyers choosing tiers, with an alarm that sounds (visually, no audio) when a tier starts cannibalizing the one above it. Fixing a mispriced ladder and watching the streams resort themselves is the satisfying core loop.

**The hook into Act 4.** A news ticker flashes: the economy is turning. Priya: "Your ladder is tuned for today's buyers. What happens to it when everyone suddenly has more money, or a lot less?"

---

# Act 4: When the wind changes
### Income elasticity, the macro cycle, cross-elasticity against rivals

**Dramatic question:** which tier do you bet on when the whole economy is moving, and your rival is betting the other way?

**The scene.** The macro cycle goes live as a moving ticker. Incomes rise into a boom, then fall into a recession. Your three tiers stop behaving alike. Premium demand swings violently with income. Basic barely flinches and quietly grows its share when times are hard. Darius Vane is in the market now with his own premium bulb, so his price moves your demand too.

**The gameplay.** A multi-quarter stretch where the player rotates the product mix as the cycle turns. In the boom, leaning premium pays off enormously. As the peak rolls into recession, the player who shifted toward basic survives while the one stuck in premium capacity bleeds. Each quarter shows the income-elasticity readout for each tier and the cross-price elasticity against Darius's bulb. The drama is timing: commit your mix, then watch the cycle and the rival's move revealed together.

**Concept, stated plainly.** Income elasticity measures how demand moves with income: above 1 is a luxury (premium), near 1 is a normal good (mid), near zero is income-inelastic (basic). A genuinely inferior good, whose demand actually falls as income rises, is shown as a separate example (a no-brand commodity bulb a competitor sells) so the definition stays honest and the basic tier is not mislabeled. Cross-price elasticity against a rival measures how your sales move when the rival changes price: positive means substitutes.

**Transparent formula.** Income elasticity shown live for each tier: percent change in that tier's demand divided by percent change in income, with the cycle feeding the income number. Cross-price elasticity against Darius shown the same way. The student watches premium's income elasticity print a big number while basic's prints a small one, and finally understands why the mix has to rotate.

**The thrill mechanic.** The cycle ticker plus a "mix exposure" gauge that shows how much of your fate is riding on the luxury tier. Riding the boom feels great and watching the recession catch an over-exposed rival feels even better.

**The hook into Act 5.** Reya, sober: "We have been talking as if we could make all three in any amount we like. We cannot. We have one factory, one R&D team, one marketing budget. Everything we pour into premium is something basic does not get."

---

# Act 5: Not enough to go around
### Resource allocation across the line, the production frontier, opportunity cost

**Dramatic question:** with a fixed pool of capacity, cash, and people, how much of each product should you actually make?

**The scene.** The fantasy of "make more of everything" dies here. You have one shared capacity, one R&D budget, one marketing spend. Allocating to premium means starving basic. This is the oldest idea in economics, scarcity and opportunity cost, returning at the firm's own scale.

**The gameplay.** An allocation board: sliders for capacity, R&D, and marketing across the three tiers, each pool fixed so the sliders must sum to the total. A live production frontier shows the tradeoff between, say, premium and basic output given your fixed plant: more of one is less of the other, and the curve bows because resources are not equally good at both. Live profit feedback on every move. A bottleneck alert fires when one line starves another. The teaching payoff is the equimarginal principle made tactile: keep shifting the next rupee to wherever it earns the most, until the marginal return is equal everywhere, and the game shows you that you have hit the best mix when those marginal returns line up.

**Concept, stated plainly.** A production possibility frontier shows the combinations of two goods you can make with fixed resources, and its slope is the opportunity cost of one in terms of the other. The equimarginal principle says you allocate a scarce resource optimally when its marginal return is the same in every use. The constraint has a shadow price: what one more unit of capacity would be worth to you.

**Transparent formula.** The marginal return per unit of resource for each tier, shown live (extra profit from one more unit of capacity given to premium, to mid, to basic). The optimum is visible as the moment those three numbers meet. The shadow price of capacity prints alongside, which sets up a callback to Level 1's NPV: is it worth investing to expand the constraint?

**The thrill mechanic.** The frontier curve plus a live "leaving profit unclaimed" readout that only hits zero when your marginal returns are balanced. Chasing it to zero is a genuine optimization puzzle with immediate feedback.

**The hook into the finale.** All the pieces are on the board now. Priya: "Segments, tiers, the cycle, the budget. Next quarter it all goes live at once, and we will not be the only ones playing."

---

# Finale: The segmented war
### Everything at once, plus the regulator

**Dramatic question:** can you read the segments, rotate the mix through the cycle, price the ladder to self-select, and squeeze the surplus, all while rivals fight back and a regulator watches?

**The scene.** A multi-quarter competitive run with all three tiers, segmented demand, the macro cycle turning, and Darius Vane plus other AI firms making their own mix and pricing calls. New on the board: the Competition Commission. If you push price discrimination too far, gouge the inelastic segment, and pile up deadweight loss, you trip a regulatory threshold and eat a price cap or a fine. The welfare idea from Act 1 comes back not as a diagram but as a live constraint with teeth.

**The gameplay.** Each quarter: read the segment data, set your three prices and your mix, allocate your fixed resources, then submit and watch the market clear against rivals whose moves were hidden. Profit, surplus captured, survival through the recession, and your standing with the regulator all move together. The composite score rewards the player who captured a lot of the surplus while keeping the market healthy enough that the regulator stayed asleep.

**Concept callback.** This is the synthesis. Consumer surplus and deadweight loss from Act 1 set the regulator's trigger. Price discrimination from Acts 2 and 3 is how you capture surplus. Income and cross elasticity from Act 4 drive the mix through the cycle. Resource allocation from Act 5 funds it. The closing lesson is the level's whole moral: market power is profit, but unchecked it shrinks the pie and invites the state.

**Score.** Composite of total profit, surplus captured, recession survival, and regulatory standing, with a learning report that names which concept each decision exercised, in the same style as Level 1.

## How it bolts onto Level 1

- Same save and identity. A Level 2 unlock flag the instructor controls, so a class finishes and discusses Level 1 first.
- Same instructor pacing model: each Level 2 act is gated the same way the Level 1 acts are, so you can run it act by act with discussion between.
- Same transparent-math and reinforcement-card pattern, so it feels like one game, not two.
- The Level 1 competitive engine is the seed for the finale engine, extended to three products and segmented demand.

---

## What I would cut if the build runs long

In priority order, keep the top, drop from the bottom:

1. Act 1 welfare and Act 3 versioning are the heart. Never cut these.
2. Act 4 income elasticity and the macro cycle is the drama engine. Keep it.
3. Act 5 allocation is the most distinctive advanced idea. Keep it if at all possible.
4. Act 2 first-degree discrimination can shrink to a single thought-experiment screen if time is tight.
5. The regulator in the finale is the first thing to simplify (from a live constraint to a post-game commentary) if the engine gets too heavy.

---

## The honest size of this

This is a second project on the scale of Level 1, not an extension. The multi-product market-clearing engine, segmented demand, the self-selection model, and the welfare math are each real work, and the design rule that protects clarity (one moving part per act) is also the thing that makes it buildable. If you want to proceed, the right first step is to build Act 1 alone, end to end, prove the welfare visual reads cleanly and the transparent-math principle survives, and only then commit to the rest.
